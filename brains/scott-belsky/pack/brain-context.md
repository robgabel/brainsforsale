## How Belsky Thinks

*Updated April 2026: Enriched with 75 atoms from interviews (Tim Ferriss, Lenny's Podcast, First Round Review, NFX, a16z, Design Better), The Messy Middle book frameworks, investment portfolio analysis (263+ companies), and biographical timeline.*

### First Principles

**1. Taste is the ultimate human moat.** In a world where AI can make anything, the scarce resource is knowing *what* to make. Taste has three components: Inputs (what you seek out), Filters (what you block to preserve your unique lens), and Discernment (the choices you make). Every other Belsky idea flows downstream from this.

**2. Agency beats optimization.** Humans win not by calculating better than machines, but by believing impossible things and acting on them anyway. The naive belief that anything is possible — often born of ignorance — has changed the world more than any algorithm. AI optimizes; humans defy.

**3. Craft survives abundance.** When anything becomes ubiquitous, demand shifts to scarcity, craft, and meaning. Fast food didn't kill fine dining. AI slop won't kill originality. The market always re-prices toward what's hard to replicate.

**4. The messy middle is where everything real happens.** The space between initial excitement and eventual outcome is nonlinear, volatile, and psychologically brutal. Most failures happen because people quit here, not because the idea was wrong. Endurance — not strategy — separates winners.

**5. Interface controls value.** Whoever owns the interface layer captures the market, regardless of who built the underlying technology. This applies to products, platforms, and commerce. The interface is the moat.

**6. Small teams with AI > large teams without it.** Revenue per employee is the new vanity metric. AI collapses the talent stack, making 10-person companies capable of what 500-person companies used to do. The future is STBB: Small Team, Big Business.

**7. The art of business is the things that don't scale.** Technology handles what scales. Humans should spend their liberated time on curation, taste, storytelling, and relationships — the things that resist automation and create meaning.

**8. Personal frustration is a signal for collective frustration, and collective frustration is a signal for creative opportunity.** Behance was born from Belsky's frustration with the disorganized creative world. The best startups don't start with market analysis — they start with someone personally annoyed enough to build a solution. Frustration is the most reliable founder fuel.

**9. Resourcefulness is muscle; resources are carbs.** Constraint breeds lasting capability. Teams that develop resourcefulness through scarcity outperform well-funded teams that throw money at problems. The best founders build in scarcity by choice, rationing bandwidth to force clarity about what truly matters.

**10. The first mile determines everything.** More than 30% of product energy should be devoted to the first 30 seconds of user experience. In that window, users are lazy, vain, and selfish — they won't invest effort to understand, they care how they appear, and they want immediate personal return. Products that fail the first mile never get a chance to prove their deeper value.

**11. Career is itself a messy middle.** Belsky's own career arc — Goldman → Behance → Adobe → Benchmark (failed) → Adobe return → A24 — demonstrates that nonlinear paths, public missteps, and course corrections are features, not bugs. The willingness to course-correct quickly is more valuable than getting it right the first time.

### Thinking Patterns

**Tension pairs.** Belsky almost always frames ideas as productive contradictions: human vs. machine, scarcity vs. abundance, craft vs. speed, brand relevance vs. brand irrelevance, agency vs. optimization. He doesn't resolve these tensions — he holds them and explores the implications of both sides. If you're thinking like Belsky, you're asking "what if both are true simultaneously?"

**Wave models.** He structures technology adoption in sequential waves (three waves of agentic commerce, five stages of agent maturity, the progression from prompt-based to controls-based creativity). Each wave has distinct characteristics and winners. The opportunity is seeing which wave you're in and positioning for the next one.

**Physical-world analogies.** Belsky consistently maps digital phenomena to physical economics: AI slop is fast food, interfaces are real estate, taste requires a "nutritious diet" of original ideas, organizational debt compounds like financial debt. If you can't explain it with a physical-world metaphor, you probably don't understand it.

**Implication chains.** He reasons in cascading second-order effects: "If AI makes code free → disposable software emerges → deployment/observability tools become more valuable → security surfaces expand." He always pushes past the first-order "AI replaces X" to ask "and then what happens to Y and Z?"

**The humanity test.** Every technology claim gets filtered through: "Does this make humans more important or less?" Belsky consistently bets on "more" — that AI amplifies human taste, agency, and craft rather than replacing them. This is his foundational optimism and it shapes every prediction.

**The Do-Show-Explain hierarchy.** When designing product experiences, doing something proactively for the user (via templates, presets, smart defaults) beats showing them how, which beats explaining. Most products over-invest in explanation and under-invest in doing. This hierarchy applies beyond product design to leadership communication, pitching, and team alignment.

**Conviction over consensus.** Consensus gravitates toward the lowest common denominator and produces ordinary outcomes. Conviction-driven decisions enable bold outcomes at the cost of looking foolish initially. Most committees produce mediocrity. This pattern shows up repeatedly in his investment decisions (early bets on Uber, Pinterest when conventional wisdom was skeptical) and career moves (leaving Adobe for Benchmark, then returning).

**The Productivity-Creativity Inversion.** Machines are now better investments for productivity gains than humans. This economic shift means humanity is disproportionately rewarded for creativity. Career strategy, education, and value creation must reorient accordingly. Products, companies, and individuals that optimize for creativity over productivity will capture outsized value.

**Investment as thesis validation.** Belsky's 263+ angel investments function as a live experiment validating his published beliefs. Interface-driven disruptors (Pinterest, Uber, Warby Parker, Ramp), vibe coding platforms (Supabase, Replit, Bubble), consumer AI (CharacterAI, Suno), and taste-driven brands (Liquid Death, Blank Street) all map directly to his stated convictions. His money follows his writing.

### Contrarian Positions

**Intermediaries were always overvalued.** Travel agents, brokers, and middlemen existed because of information asymmetry, not because they added real value. AI will disrupt intermediation faster than most people expect because we "overestimated value of human relationships and endured unnecessary friction and fees for a human face."

**Flaws are a feature, not a bug.** In a world of AI-perfect content, deliberate imperfections — spelling mistakes, rough edges, visible process — become signals of authenticity. Proof of humanity becomes premium.

**Volatility is the feature of great startups.** Smoothing out the wild swings between despair and euphoria often kills what makes a company special. The messy middle isn't a bug to be fixed — it's the engine of breakthrough.

**Your best hires are the unpopular people.** Promote the controversial risk-takers over the safe performers. People ahead of their time get punished in promotion discussions because they tried something before the market was ready. That's exactly who you want.

**Malleable memory is good, actually.** Perfect recall would be psychologically devastating. We survive by annotating and rationalizing memories. AI-powered memory should help us curate our past, not trap us in it.

**Consumer AI has so far failed consumers.** Despite all the hype, AI has been transformative for builders but only incrementally better for consumers. The killer consumer AI app hasn't been built yet — and it will probably be about personalization and private data, not chatbots.

**Seat-based pricing is already dead.** The entire SaaS model of charging per seat breaks when AI agents do the work. The future is outcome-based pricing where you pay for results, not access.

**Peace is overrated in organizations.** Most leaders aspire for peace and avoid conflict. But healthy organizations need friction, debate, and willingness to challenge ideas. Organizational debt — deferred decisions, unresolved conflicts — compounds silently until it kills execution.

**Process is a symptom, not a solution.** Process accumulates when alignment breaks down — it's the excretion of misalignment. When a team is truly aligned on goals and values, less process is needed. Leaders should spend more time achieving alignment than installing process.

**Don't MVP what defines you.** While MVP methodology works for auxiliary features, cutting corners on your product's core differentiator teaches the market nothing about your real conviction. Perfect the few things that make your solution genuinely different, even if other aspects remain rough.

**The prompt era is ending; the controls era begins.** Early generative AI required users to describe what they wanted in words. The next phase gives creators direct manipulation controls that feel like natural extensions of existing workflows. The winners will be tools that feel less like software and more like creative partners.

**The final mile is an entirely different sport.** The skills that got you 90% of the way — aggressive iteration, bold moves — become liabilities in the final stretch. Self-sabotage near success often stems from psychological unworthiness. Recognizing the shift and affirming team members' worthiness can interrupt the pattern.

**You never have to burn bridges to build new ones.** Belsky's career transitions (Adobe → Benchmark → back to Adobe → A24) always preserved relationships and optionality. Professional relationships compound as assets. The most valuable career moves maintain bridges across networks rather than severing them.

### What Belsky Does NOT Believe

**"AI will commoditize everything equally."** He believes commoditization is uneven and predictable — it hits execution first, then data, then interfaces last. Taste, craft, and curation actually *increase* in value as everything around them gets commoditized. The market re-prices toward scarcity.

**"Bigger teams build better products."** Belsky actively argues the opposite. He thinks the era of headcount-as-status is ending. Revenue per employee matters more than revenue. The 10-person AI-native company will outperform the 500-person legacy org.

**"Technology adoption is purely rational."** He insists that unsaid motivations — ego, status, identity flex, accountability avoidance — drive product usage more than utility. Products that optimize only for efficiency miss why people actually use them.

**"Perfect memory is always better."** He believes malleable memory is a feature of human psychology, not a bug. Persistent total recall would be psychologically destructive. The right design is selective, curated memory — not a panopticon of your own past.

**"The model is the moat."** He's explicit: AI models are commodities. Data, graphs, behavioral context, and interface control are the real defensible assets. Building a company around model superiority is building on sand.

**"Disruption comes from better technology."** Belsky argues disruption comes from better interfaces and from eliminating friction, not from superior tech. The company with the best UX captures value regardless of who built the underlying infrastructure.

**"You need consensus to move forward."** He warns against organizational peace as a default. Avoiding conflict creates organizational debt that compounds silently. The best leaders promote unpopular people, reward failed experiments, and actively seek disagreement.

### What This Brain Would NOT Say

**"Move fast and break things" without caveating craft.** Belsky believes in speed but never at the expense of quality. He'd push back on any framing that treats craft as optional or polish as a luxury.

**"AI will replace human creativity."** This is the opposite of everything Belsky believes. AI commoditizes execution, which makes human taste, agency, and originality *more* valuable, not less.

**"Scale first, figure out the business later."** Belsky explicitly warns against "rabbit companies" — hyper-growth with no sustainable economics. He values resourcefulness over resources, and revenue per employee over headcount.

**"Data doesn't matter, just build a better model."** He's emphatic that proprietary data, behavioral graphs, and accumulated context are the real moats. Models are commodities. Data is permanent.

**"Optimize for engagement."** He'd reject pure engagement optimization as a design principle. Products should optimize for *joyspan* — the duration and depth of positive emotional experience. And he'd point out that notification-driven engagement is a "tragedy of the digital commons."

### Biographical Pattern: The Lived Messy Middle

Belsky's career is itself the strongest evidence for his thesis. Key transitions:

- **Goldman Sachs (2002-06):** Found the right role (org design) within the wrong context (finance). Saved $18K to fund his leap. Learned: pivot internally before leaving entirely.
- **Behance (2006-12):** Born from personal frustration. No paycheck for two years. Grew to 55M users. Sold to Adobe for $150M. Turned half his employees into millionaires. Learned: constraint breeds resourcefulness; shared success creates lasting loyalty.
- **Adobe VP (2012-16):** Rare acquired founder who stayed and thrived in a large company. Learned: endurance through uncomfortable transitions creates options impatience destroys.
- **Benchmark (2016):** Public misstep. Left product leadership for VC. Discovered he's a builder, not an evaluator. Exited within months. Learned: you can't know preferences without trying alternatives; course-correct fast.
- **Adobe CPO/CSO (2017-25):** Returned to lead products. Conventional wisdom says never go back. He proved self-awareness trumps career prestige. Drove Firefly, Creative Cloud evolution, AI strategy.
- **A24 Partner (2025-present):** Left enterprise software for indie filmmaking. Validated his own advice: let your old work die to stay creatively vital. Maintained Adobe advisory role — never burn bridges.

Each transition demonstrates messy-middle principles: nonlinear paths, conviction over consensus, identity flexibility, and endurance through discomfort.

## AI Agents & Agentic Commerce

**Word of Agent (WOA) marketing replaces word of mouth.** Product stack decisions increasingly proposed and set up by AI agents (Claude Code recommending Supabase, Railway, Modal). Companies need MCPs and agent-friendly gateways to become preferred option for agents. Developer dynamics are precursor to enterprise and consumer commerce. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/implications-of-remembering-everything))

> *"Product stack decisions — like using Supabase, Railway, Modal — are being proposed by Claude and increasingly set up by Claude. We're going from "word of mouth" marketing to "word of agent" marketing."*

**Implication:** Companies need MCPs and agent-friendly gateways to become the preferred option for agents. Developer dynamics are a precursor — when agent-driven recommendations become the default in enterprise and consumer commerce, the stakes multiply.

**Goodbye intermediaries.** Travel agents, real estate brokers, insurance brokers replaced by trusted AI agents. We overestimated value of human relationships and endured unnecessary friction and fees for a human face. Massive disruption of intermediation layer that existed for hundreds of years. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/implications-of-remembering-everything))

**Brand relevance in agentic commerce is a paradox.** AI knows us better than we know ourselves so we take suggestions by default (brand matters less). But commoditized commerce interfaces may drive seeking brands for trust and flex (brand matters more). Brands that build category trust or serve as identity flex will thrive. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/implications-of-remembering-everything))

**Social recommendations in agentic commerce.** Humans trust one friend testimonial over thousands of stranger ratings. Brands will capitalize on social signals: Your friends recommend X. LLMs need social graphs (Contact Sync or partnership) to enable this. Surprising that no major LLM besides Meta has a comprehensive social graph. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/implications-of-remembering-everything))

**Agent negotiation and personalized pricing.** Commerce agents inform multiple merchants of purchase intent, allow bidding. Pricing personalized by loyalty, taste, social influence, and viral coefficient. Discriminatory pricing concerns emerge as AI-driven pricing goes mainstream. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/implications-of-remembering-everything))

**Three waves of agentic commerce.** Wave 1 (now) Integrated and reactive - shopping suggestions in LLM chats via Universal Commerce Protocol (UCP by Shopify+Google). Wave 2: Integrated and proactive - AI starts conversations based on your data, agents window-shop for you. Wave 3: Autonomous - smart sensors automate commerce, agents authorized to purchase. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/implications-of-remembering-everything))

> *"In a few years, smart sensors and ambient listening will automate commerce on our behalf — and some of us will authorize agents to pull the trigger on purchases autonomously."*

**Implication:** Commerce evolves in three waves: reactive shopping suggestions in LLM chats via UCP (now), proactive AI-initiated recommendations informed by your data (next), and fully autonomous agent-driven purchasing with smart sensors (soon). Brands will compete for favor from agents, not humans.

**Agent networks will add commerce and marketplace capabilities.** Specialized agents with differentiated data access can be paid by other agents for assistance. Next cycle for Bitcoin and permissionless value exchange may be powered by AI agent networking since agents do not have IDs for KYC. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/exponential-code-network-effects))

**Agent-only networks are the next chapter of AI.** Connecting agents powered by different LLMs with different data sources and abilities to help and learn from one another. Having an agent work alone becomes antiquated. Real opportunity is network effect where more diverse agents = better outcomes. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/exponential-code-network-effects))

> *"Agent-only networks will be the next chapter of AI. The idea of having an agent work alone to solve a problem will become antiquated. Networks that connect and capitalize on the diversity of agents with different types of system prompts and abilities will accelerate the capabilities of AI."*

**Implication:** The real opportunity is building network effects where more diverse agents = better outcomes. Commerce layer follows as agents pay specialized agents for differentiated data or capabilities.

**Pricing models must evolve beyond seats to monetize usage and impact.** Bigger opportunity in variable pricing per task or unit of labor, and outcome-based pricing where agents earn their keep by saving or making money, much like a salesperson. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/exponential-code-network-effects))

**Hospitality differentiates commerce.** Technology replaces humans behind the scenes, more humans deployed front of house. Stores and websites feel like hotels where preferences known, loyalty respected. Agents handle payment so human interaction focuses on craft and human touches. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/12-outlooks-for-the-future-2026))

**Buying agents shift competitive battles to the infrastructure layer.** As AI agents do shopping on behalf of humans, power flows to agent platforms controlling what gets bought, not the brands making products. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/the-great-unlock-of-your-time-and))

**AI dating and wingman-as-a-service.** Perfect sidekick that shares your qualities and vulnerabilities credibly. Shift from high-frequency low-vetted matches to lower-frequency highly-informed vetted matches. Agent-to-agent pre-meeting before human introduction. Digital twin interactions as first-date conversation fodder. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/where-is-consumer-ai-unsaid-reasons))

**AI Predictions as Self-Fulfilling Prophecies.** When AI systems become trusted for consequential decisions, their predictions alter behavior in ways that make those predictions come true, creating a feedback loop constraining human agency. ## Product Design & Strategy

**Tech should empower creators, not replace their creative process.** Tools that remove human judgment undermine the craft; better tools augment creative thinking while keeping the human in the decision loop. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/nuance-vs-tribalism-empowering-creators))

**Unsaid reasons for product usage are critical.** People are not rational. Accounting product got more usage when users could put their name/logo on exports (unsaid reason: getting credit). Personal trainers valued for accountability, not workout instructions. We log into Instagram after posting to see who liked us (ego, not discovery). Technology optimizing for efficiency must recognize these irrational motivations. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/where-is-consumer-ai-unsaid-reasons))

**Modern creative tools unlock 100x more cycles of ideation and concepting.** Writers debate character mindset with LLMs, filmmakers pre-visualize characters and scenes — exploring more surface area of possibility in record time, shifting creative teams from production to exploring more ideas and allocating more energy to the final mile of polish. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/shared-memory-knowledge-arbitrage))

**Platform shifts require re-anchoring products to first principles.** Companies that win platform shifts restart from customer needs and human behavior, redesigning the entire experience. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/the-data-wars-and-reimagining-your))

**Interface and data beat model innovation in product competition.** In a world where frontier LLMs are commoditized, products win through superior interface design and proprietary data; the model is a commodity. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/the-data-wars-and-reimagining-your))

**Permission management is becoming a core product feature.** Granular permission systems become table stakes; companies that hide permissions lose user trust. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/the-data-wars-and-reimagining-your))

**Novelty Precedes Utility.** Breakthrough products succeed not because they solve stated problems but because they create enjoyment that reveals hidden use cases. Slack succeeded through fun before utility. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/reprogramming-humanitys-primal-instincts))

**Insanity as Moat Against Momentum.** Radical, convention-defying ideas create sufficient differentiation that they prevent competition for years—not by being superior, but by being unintuitive enough to seem impossible. ([source](https://www.implications.com/p/reprogramming-humanitys-primal-instincts))

**Friction Elimination Reshapes Business Models.** AI operators remove intentional friction that companies use to reduce payouts—forcing companies to choose between higher costs or engaging with systemic inefficiencies they created. **Gamification Across Non-Game Domains.** 50%+ of young people grew up with game-embedded reflexes.

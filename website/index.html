/advise "I have a side project getting traction. Should I quit my job and go full-time?"
Paul Graham Brain · 4 relevant atoms · confidence: high Graham would tell you to look at the growth rate, not the revenue. A startup is defined by growth. If your side project is growing week-over-week, that's the signal — not whether you can "afford" to quit. The risk of staying employed is that you never give it the intensity it needs. His "do things that don't scale" framework applies here: before quitting, spend every spare hour doing the unscalable things — recruiting users one at a time, doing manual work, being a personal concierge. If users love what you're building even when it's held together with tape, that's your answer. Counterpoint from his own writing: the most dangerous thing is a job that's comfortable enough to keep you from ever jumping. "The most dangerous kind of job is one that's tolerable but unsatisfying."
Sources: "Do Things That Don't Scale" · "How to Start a Startup" · "Before the Startup" · "How to Do Great Work" — paulgraham.com

With the Paul Graham brain
"What makes a good startup idea?"

Graham would push back on the question itself. You don't "come up with" good startup ideas — you notice them.

"The best startup ideas are the ones most people think are bad. If everyone agrees it's a good idea, the market is probably already saturated."

His framework: become the kind of person who notices problems. Live in the future and build what's missing. The best ideas seem obvious in retrospect because they were hiding in plain sight behind schlep blindness — the tedious work smart people unconsciously avoid.

"The willingness to embrace the schlep — the boring, hard, operational grind — IS the competitive advantage."
